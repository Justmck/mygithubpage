The DailyUI Challenge

This challenge is a series of design challenges in the user interface space, asking those who take it on to create UI elements independent of any additional planning. 5 days a week, Monday-Friday, participants are emailed a prompt and must complete it by the end of the day. The point of the challenge is to become a better UI designer through tons of practice. It's a challenge I highly reccommend, and after looking into it I decided to start at the beginning of the new year albeit with a few changes, primarily being that I would only spend an hour per project. Being a full-time student is quite time consuming on it's own, and each prompt as easy as it might seem could easily take me an entire day. So, I decided put a time constraint for myself to not only practice interface design techniques, but also rapid ideation and prototyping during a time crunch.

Why I Decided to do it

This challenge has been met with it's fair share of criticism. It has been said that the "solutions" to the prompts addressed aren't real solutions, and are just pretty pixels without any thought put into them, and that this challenge contributes to a phenomenon called the dribbblisation of design. I disagree. I believe that the challenge lets participants explore different visual styles and experiment with ideas that while not necessarily useful, can help contribute to projects in the future. Design is about communicating ideas, and if you can practice the skills needed to convey those ideas, then practicing is what you should do. So while my Daily UI "shots" aren't sometimes realistic, I've added them to my portfolio as a way to show off my expanding visual UI design talent and expand my techniques so that when a larger, substantial project comes around, I'll be better prepared for it.


1 hour a day. 100 days. Let's see what happens.

Lessons I've Learned

1. Work with, not against constraints.
2. If the clock is ticking down, pick something and stick with it; don't get distracted by "what if I had..."
3. Iterate. Iterate. Iterate.
4. Take the number of affordances you have, and double it; it's not enough.
